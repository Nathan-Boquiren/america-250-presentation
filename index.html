Act 1

In 1492, Christopher Columbus crossed the Atlantic Ocean, which opened the doors to a world Europe had never seen before. Over the next few centuries, explorers, colonists, and pilgrims sailed to America to start new lives in the New World.

Britain established thirteen colonies along the east coast. And as they flourished and grew, many of the colonists developed a new identity, outside of being British. An identity that was distinctly American.

However, Britain still saw them as subjects of the Crown. So, it unfairly governed, controlled and taxed the colonists. To the people who no longer saw themselves as British, that was unacceptable.

This frustration with the British accumulated over time, and after multiple skirmishes, petitions, and acts of protest, the colonists finally had enough.

On July 4th, 1776, 250 years ago, fifty-six delegates signed the Declaration of Independence. This declaration not only established a new nation, but a nation “conceived in liberty, and dedicated to the proposition that all men are created equal.”

After the declaration, war ensued. The ragtag Continental Army, led by George Washington, went up against the strongest military in the world. After 8 years of war, the American Patriots won, and the United States became an independent country.

Four years later, the Founding Fathers drafted the Constitution, which established the way we govern our country even today. To lead the new nation, George Washington was unanimously elected its first President in 1789.

The years that followed saw the rise of a nation unlike anything the world had ever seen. One that is arguably the greatest country on earth, the United States of America.

Act 2

The next fifty years, from 1826 to 1876, were defined by ambition and conflict. With the founding generation gone, Americans turned their eyes west, pushing across the Great Plains all the way to the Pacific.

But in 1861, that momentum was halted. The Civil War split the nation in two, pitting Americans against Americans in the bloodiest conflict in the country's history.

Abraham Lincoln held the Union together. When the war ended in 1865, slavery was abolished, and America emerged from its greatest trial intact.

Four years later, the Transcontinental Railroad connected the country from coast to coast. A lesser nation would not have survived that fifty years. This one thrived.
