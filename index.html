Act 1

In 1492, Christopher Columbus crossed the Atlantic Ocean, which opened the doors to a world Europe had never seen before. Over the next few centuries, explorers, colonists, and pilgrims sailed to America to start new lives in the New World.

Britain founded thirteen colonies along the east coast. And as they flourished and grew, many of the colonists developed a new identity, outside of being British. An identity that was distinctly American.

However, Britain still saw them as subjects of the Crown. So, it unfairly governed, controlled and taxed the colonists. To the people who no longer saw themselves as British, that was unacceptable.

This frustration with the British accumulated over time, and after multiple skirmishes, petitions, and acts of protest, the colonists finally had enough.

On July 4th, 1776, 250 years ago, fifty-six delegates signed the Declaration of Independence. This declaration not only established a new nation, but a nation “conceived in liberty", and in the idea that "all men are created equal", endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable rights.

After the declaration, war ensued. The ragtag Continental Army, led by George Washington, went up against the strongest military in the world. After 8 years of war, the American Patriots won, and the United States became an independent country.

Four years later, the Founding Fathers drafted the Constitution, which defined the way we govern our country even today. To lead the new nation, George Washington was unanimously elected its first President in 1789.

The years that followed saw the rise of a nation unlike anything the world had ever seen. One that is arguably the greatest country on earth, the United States of America.

Act 2

From 1826, the next fifty years were defined by Manifest Destiny and national conflict. Pioneers like Lewis and Clark explored and charted the path across the western wilderness. Hundreds of thousands of settlers followed, pushing all the way to the Pacific.

But, in 1861, that momentum was halted. The Civil War split the nation in two, pitting Americans against Americans. At the Battle of Gettysburg alone, over 50,000 Americans died in just three days. It was the bloodiest battle in the country's history.

President Abraham Lincoln masterfully held the Union together. His Emancipation Proclamation in 1863 began the end of slavery. When the war finally ended, over 600,000 Americans had fallen, but the nation emerged united as one.

Four years later, the Transcontinental Railroad was completed, and connected the country from coast to coast. The Reconstruction had begun, and for America, unprecedented greatness lay ahead.

Act 3

From 1876 to 1926, America made its debut on the world stage with innovation and strength.

Inventors revolutionized everyday life, such as Alexander Graham Bell and the telephone, and Edison with the lightbulb.

Transportation was also transformed when the Wright brothers invented the airplane and Henry Ford's moving assembly line made automobiles more affordable.

In 1898, Theodore Roosevelt led the Rough Riders, up San Juan Hill in the Spanish-American War. The victory made him a national hero, and in 1901, he became the youngest President ever.

As President, Roosevelt expanded the US navy, began construction of the Panama Canal, and established America as a rising power on the global stage.

In 1917, the US entered the First World War. Over two million American soldiers deployed to Europe, and fought in the trenches of France as the Allies pushed toward victory.

When the war ended in 1918, the United States positioned itself as one of the most powerful nations. Fifty years of invention and sacrifice had transformed the growing nation into a global power.

Act 4

In the 50 years following 1926, America endured some of its greatest hardships, but we also achieved some of our greatest triumphs.

In 1929, the stock market crashed, and the nation plunged into the Great Depression. Thirty million Americans lost their livelihoods, and it took a decade for the country to recover.

Then, in 1941, Japan attacked Pearl Harbor, and the United States entered the Second World War. Millions of soldiers deployed to fight in Europe and the Pacific.

As the war spread across the globe, the free world was threatened, and America fought back. On June 6, 1944, American troops stormed the beaches of Normandy on D-Day, an operation that turned the tide against Nazi Germany.

The war ended in 1945, and the United States became the most powerful nation on Earth. But peace soon gave way to a new rivalry, as America and the Soviet Union entered the Cold War.

That rivalry launched the space race. In 1961, President Kennedy pledged that America would land a man on the Moon, and in 1969, Neil Armstrong took the first steps on its surface.

Fifty years of hardship, war, and ambition had proven, once again, that no obstacle was too great for the United States of America.

Act 5

In the last 50 years, America closed out one battle and opened an era of growth unlike any before it.

In 1987, President Reagan stood in Berlin and demanded, "Tear down this wall." Two years later, the Berlin wall fell, and the Soviet Union collapsed. The Cold War was won.

Our innovation then reshaped the entire world. The personal computer, the internet, and the smartphone all emerged from American ingenuity.

More recently, visionaries like Elon Musk helped pioneer reusable rockets and self-driving electric vehicles, opening the door to humanity's next frontier.

In 2020, the country faced a global pandemic that tested every American family. And once again, the country endured, adapted, and came out the other side.

In 2024, Americans elected Donald J. Trump for a second term, a political comeback the likes of which the world has never seen. He returned to the White House determined to restore American strength at home and abroad, and he did.

After 250 years, the United States is still a young nation. Yet, in that short time, it rose to become the greatest country the world has ever known, achieving in two and a half centuries what other empires took millennia to build.

Today, we are still the land of the free and the home of the brave, a nation where the American Dream is alive and still worth fighting for. A nation where you can come from a different country, assimilate, and call yourself an American. So here's to 250 years of independence and liberty, and 250 more. God bless the United States of America! The best is yet to come.
